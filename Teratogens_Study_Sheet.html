🔬 Key Principles of Teratology

What is a teratogen?Any environmental agent that interferes with prenatal development — substances, infections, radiation, or chemicals.
Critical periodMost damage occurs in the embryonic period (weeks 3–8) when organ systems are forming. Timing = the most important variable.
Dose–responseHigher dose generally = greater harm. Some teratogens (e.g. thalidomide) cause damage at very low doses.
Placental barrierNot a true barrier — most teratogens cross the placenta. Some concentrate in the fetus at levels higher than the mother.
Paternal effectsFathers can also contribute via damaged sperm chromosomes (e.g. alcohol, chemicals), causing miscarriage or genetic defects.

💊 Substances

4 agents
Caffeine
Legal stimulant — coffee, tea, sodas, energy drinks
⚠️ Some countries advise complete avoidance
Risk threshold>4 cups/sodas per day MechanismInfluences blood pressure; crosses placenta rapidly CategoryLegal substance — widely consumed Country noteNetherlands advises complete avoidance during pregnancy
  • Miscarriage risk at high intake levels
  • Low birth weight in newborns
  • Blood pressure elevation — dangerous for pregnant women
  • Limit to <200 mg/day (approx. 1–2 cups coffee) per most guidelines
  • Netherlands: complete abstinence recommended
  • Avoid energy drinks and high-caffeine sodas
★ Caffeine: threshold effect — moderate amounts considered lower risk, but dose above 4 cups/day linked to miscarriage & low birth weight. Note the Netherlands advises full avoidance — a contrast to other national guidelines.
Smoking / Nicotine ★
Tobacco; also e-cigarettes and nicotine patches
⚠️ Potentiates other teratogens · SIDS risk
MechanismImpairs placental function — reduces oxygen & nutrient exchange ConcentrationDose is HIGHER in infant than mother (rapid diffusion through placenta) Dose effectHigher exposure = higher miscarriage risk & lower birth weight SynergyPotentiates other teratogens by increasing their placental transport
  • Fetal growth retardation & low-birth-weight deliveries
  • Higher incidence of ectopic pregnancies (zygote in fallopian tube)
  • Sudden Infant Death Syndrome (SIDS)
  • Elevated fetal heart rate (nicotinic receptor stimulation → adrenaline release)
  • Disrupted autonomic nervous system development
  • Complete cessation is strongly advised
  • NRT (patches, gum) under medical guidance if cessation difficult
  • Avoid secondhand smoke environments
★ Exam Q: How does smoking affect the child's heart rate?

Nicotine crosses the placenta and reaches HIGHER concentrations in the fetus than in the mother. It acts as a stimulant on the autonomic nervous system — binding to nicotinic acetylcholine receptors and triggering adrenaline (epinephrine) release → elevated fetal heart rate.

Chronic exposure disrupts normal development of the fetal autonomic nervous system, impairing heart rate regulation even after birth — a key mechanism linked to SIDS.

Alcohol ★
FAS · FAE · paternal effects
⚠️ No safe level established — whole pregnancy
Indirect harmCompromises placental functioning throughout whole pregnancy Direct harmStructural brain anomalies; risk of miscarriage Paternal effectsReduced sperm motility & count, abnormal sperm → lower birth weight in child OutcomesFAS (severe) and FAE (milder) — both irreversible
  • Fetal Alcohol Syndrome (FAS) — heavy/regular drinking
  • Fetal Alcohol Effects (FAE) — lower/less consistent exposure
  • Miscarriage and premature delivery
  • Structural brain abnormalities; heart defects
  • Paternal alcohol use linked to lower birth weight in offspring
FAS — Severe (heavy/regular drinking)
PHYSICAL: Microcephaly, flattened philtrum, thin upper lip, widely-spaced eyes, low birth weight, heart defects
MENTAL: Intellectual disability, impulse control deficits, attention deficits (ADHD-like), poor memory, impaired abstract reasoning
FAE — Milder (lower/less consistent)
PHYSICAL: Features subtle or absent; growth issues less pronounced
MENTAL: Learning difficulties, attention & memory problems, behavioral issues — without full FAS physical profile
★ Exam Q: Physical & mental characteristics of FAS vs FAE
FAS requires all 3 criteria:
  • Growth deficiency
  • Facial abnormalities (microcephaly, philtrum, thin lip, small eyes)
  • CNS dysfunction (intellectual disability, impulse/attention deficits)
FAE = some but not all criteria:
  • Primarily cognitive & behavioral problems
  • Subtle or absent physical features
Marijuana / Cannabis
THC crosses placenta; effects especially in boys
⚠️ Amygdala effects on emotional behavior
Brain targetBasal nucleus of the amygdala — regulates emotional behavior Sex differenceEffects especially pronounced in boys MechanismTHC and metabolites cross the placenta and accumulate in fetal tissue
  • Tremors in first weeks of life
  • Sleep disturbances in newborns
  • Lack of interest in surroundings (reduced arousal)
  • Long-term emotional regulation difficulties
  • Effects especially pronounced in boys
  • Complete avoidance recommended during pregnancy
  • No established safe level
  • Risk increases with frequency and potency of use
★ Marijuana = amygdala (emotional behavior) is the key brain structure affected. Remember: effects are especially pronounced in boys — a sex-specific vulnerability. Early signs: tremors, sleep disturbances, and reduced interest in surroundings in the newborn.

💉 Medications

4 agents
Anti-depressants ★
SSRIs · Lithium · Mood stabilizers
⚠️ Ethical dilemma — treated vs untreated depression
SSRIs (3rd tri)Neonatal adaptation syndrome — tremors, feeding issues, irritability Lithium (1st tri)Heart defects during 1st trimester organogenesis Ethical dilemmaUntreated depression also harms fetus via stress hormones & neglect
  • Organ development problems (various antidepressants)
  • Lithium → cardiac defects in 1st trimester
  • SSRIs in 3rd trimester → neonatal adaptation syndrome (tremors, feeding difficulties, irritability)
FOR use
Untreated severe depression → self-neglect, suicidal ideation; stress cortisol crosses placenta and damages fetal brain; rapid relapse risk if stopped
AGAINST use
Clear teratogenic risks (cardiac, neonatal adaptation); CBT may be sufficient for mild-moderate cases; 1st trimester most vulnerable
★ Exam Q: Ethical issue — when to allow / restrict?
Allow when:
  • Severe depression with suicidal risk
  • Risk to mother clearly outweighs fetal risk
  • Generally after 1st trimester (major organogenesis complete)
Restrict when:
  • Mild-to-moderate depression manageable by therapy (CBT)
  • During 1st trimester when organ formation is most vulnerable
  • Lithium & mood stabilisers → cardiac defects in 1st trimester
Thalidomide
Historical case — 1960s morning sickness drug
⚠️ Phocomelia · Critical window: weeks 3–8
EraSold in 1960s to alleviate morning sickness Key outcomePhocomelia — structural abnormalities of limbs (all/parts missing) Critical windowWeeks 3–8: limb buds forming — extremely sensitive HallmarkFeet/hands attached directly to torso (flipper-like limbs)
  • Phocomelia — limb deformities (feet attached directly to torso)
  • Deformities of eyes, ears, nose, and heart
  • Effects limited to embryonic period (weeks 3–8 of development)
  • Withdrawn from market in early 1960s after link to birth defects proven
  • Pivotal case for modern drug testing & teratology research
  • Led to stricter FDA/EMA regulations for testing drugs in pregnancy
  • Thalidomide now used under strict controls (e.g. for leprosy, myeloma)
★ Thalidomide = the classic historical teratogen case. Key terms: PHOCOMELIA (limbs missing/feet at torso). Illustrates the critical period concept — damage occurred only in weeks 3–8. Transformed how drugs are tested before approval.
Pain Medication
Aspirin · Ibuprofen (NSAIDs)
⚠️ Trimester-specific risks
AspirinGrowth retardation, poor motor control, even infant death Ibuprofen3rd trimester use → prolonged delivery & pulmonary hypertension MechanismProstaglandin inhibition affects fetal circulation and development
  • Aspirin → fetal growth retardation & poor motor control
  • Aspirin → infant death (in severe cases)
  • Ibuprofen in 3rd trimester → increased risk of prolonged delivery
  • Ibuprofen in 3rd trimester → pulmonary hypertension in newborns
  • Paracetamol (acetaminophen) is the preferred pain reliever during pregnancy
  • Avoid NSAIDs, especially in 3rd trimester
  • Low-dose aspirin may be prescribed for specific conditions (pre-eclampsia prevention) — always medical supervision
★ Aspirin → growth retardation + poor motor control. Ibuprofen (3rd trimester) → prolonged delivery + pulmonary hypertension in newborns. Remember: trimester matters — 3rd trimester is the key risk window for ibuprofen.
Diethylstilbestrol (DES)
Synthetic estrogen — prescribed 1940s–1965
⚠️ Transgenerational effects on daughters
UsePrescribed 1940s–1965 to prevent miscarriages Key findingAffected reproductive organs; rare cervical cancer (daughters) TransgenerationalDaughters of exposed mothers → higher risk of miscarriage or premature delivery
  • Reproductive organ abnormalities in exposed offspring
  • Small but elevated risk of developing rare cervical/vaginal cancer (daughters)
  • Daughters of DES mothers: increased miscarriage & premature delivery risk
  • DES was withdrawn from use in pregnant women by early 1970s
  • Classic example of delayed and transgenerational teratogenic effects
  • Important for understanding long-term consequences of prenatal exposure
★ DES = key transgenerational effect — the daughters of exposed mothers faced higher miscarriage & premature delivery risk. Demonstrates that teratogenic effects can skip to the next generation. Historical case similar to thalidomide in its policy impact.

⚠️ Illegal Drugs

2 agents
Heroin / Methadone / Opioids
Addictive narcotics — withdrawal in newborn
⚠️ 60–80% of infants face tough first month
Gross defectsNo gross physical abnormalities — but significant functional consequences Withdrawal60–80% of exposed infants experience a difficult first month due to withdrawal DurationWithdrawal symptoms can last up to 4 months
  • Higher incidence of miscarriage and premature delivery
  • Infant death risk elevated
  • Neonatal withdrawal: tremors, restlessness, sleep disturbances
  • Trouble breathing and swallowing
  • Symptoms can persist up to 4 months
  • Methadone maintenance under supervision is harm-reduction approach
  • Abrupt cessation can be dangerous — managed tapering preferred
  • Neonates require monitoring for Neonatal Opioid Withdrawal Syndrome (NOWS)
★ Heroin/opioids: note NO gross physical deformities — distinction from thalidomide. Instead: functional problems — withdrawal (60–80% affected), tremors, sleep disturbances up to 4 months. Also higher miscarriage, premature delivery, and infant death.
Cocaine ★
Vasoconstrictor — fetal oxygen deprivation
⚠️ Attachment disruption · 3 cognitive problems
MechanismConstricts blood vessels of mother & fetus → elevates fetal blood pressure, reduces O₂/nutrients AttachmentIrritable baby → bonding difficulties → insecure attachment (indirect disruption) CognitionAttention, language/verbal reasoning, and executive function deficits
  • Often causes miscarriages or premature births
  • Tremors and sleep disturbances in newborns
  • Sluggish inattention to environment
  • Indirectly disrupts parent–child attachment (irritable baby → bonding difficulties)
  • Multiple cognitive problems (attention, language, executive function)
  • No safe level during pregnancy — abstinence required
  • Substance use treatment and prenatal care are critical
  • Monitor infant for cognitive and behavioral development
★ Exam Q: Name 3 cognitive problems caused by cocaine exposure
  • Attention deficits — sustained difficulties with focused & selective attention; ADHD-like symptoms
  • Language & verbal reasoning deficits — impaired expressive & receptive language, slower vocabulary acquisition, poorer verbal memory
  • Executive function impairments — planning, working memory, cognitive flexibility, inhibitory control (prefrontal cortex disruption)

🦠 Infectious Agents

4 agents
Genital Herpes (HSV-2)
Incurable STI — transmission during delivery
⚠️ 1/3 of infected newborns die
TransmissionMost infections occur DURING BIRTH via contact with mother's genitals Prognosis1/3 of infected newborns die Survivors25–30%: blindness, brain damage, serious neurological disorders SolutionCaesarean delivery to avoid vaginal contact
  • 1/3 of infected newborns die
  • 25–30% of survivors: blindness
  • Brain damage and neurological disorders in survivors
  • Incurable — no vaccine available
  • Caesarean (C-section) delivery if active outbreak at time of birth
  • Antiviral suppression therapy (acyclovir) in late pregnancy reduces outbreak risk
  • Mothers should inform their obstetrician of HSV status
★ Herpes = transmitted during BIRTH (not in utero) — this makes it preventable via C-section. Memorise the stats: 1/3 die, 25–30% of survivors suffer blindness/brain damage. Contrast with rubella which is most dangerous EARLY in pregnancy.
Rubella (German Measles)
Timing-dependent severity — early = most dangerous
⚠️ Blindness · Deafness · Cardiac abnormalities
Critical windowEarly in pregnancy — severity decreases as pregnancy progresses Primary outcomeBlindness (hallmark of early 1st trimester infection) Key principleSeverity depends on TIMING — classic critical period example
  • Blindness — especially if infected in early pregnancy
  • Deafness
  • Cardiac abnormalities
  • Mental retardation
  • Severity decreases in later trimesters
  • MMR vaccination before pregnancy is the primary prevention
  • Vaccine not safe during pregnancy — vaccinate pre-conception
  • If exposed during pregnancy: assess immune status, consider immunoglobulin
★ Rubella = textbook critical period example. Earlier infection = worse outcomes. Blindness is the hallmark of early exposure. Deafness, cardiac defects, and mental retardation also possible. Preventable with MMR vaccine (pre-conception).
Toxoplasmosis
Parasite from cats / raw meat
⚠️ Eye & brain damage in 1st trimester
CauseSingle-celled parasite (Toxoplasma gondii) SourcesCat feces (litter boxes) · raw or undercooked meat In adultsCold-like symptoms only (often asymptomatic) 1st trimesterEye and brain damage Later pregnancyMiscarriage
  • 1st trimester: eye damage (chorioretinitis → vision loss)
  • 1st trimester: brain damage (encephalitis, calcifications)
  • Later in pregnancy: miscarriage
  • Adults: minimal symptoms (cold-like)
  • Avoid handling cat litter during pregnancy (delegate to someone else)
  • Cook all meat thoroughly (no rare/medium-rare)
  • Wash hands and vegetables thoroughly
  • Avoid stray cats; garden gloves when gardening
★ Toxoplasmosis = the pregnancy cat warning — caused by a single-celled parasite. Remember: adults get cold-like symptoms; fetus (1st trimester) gets eye & brain damage. Later in pregnancy → miscarriage. Contrast with rubella (blindness from viral infection).
Syphilis & HIV/AIDS ★
Sexually transmitted infections with placental crossing
⚠️ Syphilis most harmful after 4th month · <25% HIV transmission
Syphilis timingMost harmful AFTER 4th month — Langhans layer protects before then HIV risk<25% of babies of infected mothers born with HIV HIV survival~50% of infected babies survive past age 6 PreventionEarly antibiotic treatment (syphilis); antivirals during pregnancy (HIV)
  • Syphilis: miscarriage (if early) or congenital syphilis (bone, ear, eye, heart, brain damage)
  • HIV: many infected babies develop full-blown AIDS by age 3
  • HIV: ~50% of infected infants survive past age 6
  • Immature immune system complications — often fatal
  • Syphilis: penicillin treatment in 1st trimester prevents fetal infection entirely
  • HIV: antiretroviral therapy (ART) during pregnancy reduces transmission to <1%
  • Routine STI screening at prenatal visits is essential
★ Exam Q: When is syphilis most harmful during pregnancy?

Syphilis is most harmful after the 4th month (second trimester onward). Before this, the Langhans cell layer in the placenta prevents Treponema pallidum from crossing to the fetus.

After this layer breaks down (~16–18 weeks), bacteria can freely infect the fetus → stillbirth, congenital syphilis (bone & organ damage), neurological problems.

Early detection + penicillin in the 1st trimester can prevent fetal infection entirely.

☢️ Environmental Hazards

2 agents
Radiation ★
Ionizing radiation — X-rays, CT scans, nuclear exposure
⚠️ ALARA principle — As Low As Reasonably Achievable
Known harmsMiscarriages, disability, mental retardation UncertaintyExact extent of harm not fully known — precautionary principle applies PrincipleALARA — As Low As Reasonably Achievable
  • Miscarriages
  • Physical disability
  • Mental retardation
  • Exact dose–harm relationship still under investigation
  • Medical X-rays: postpone non-urgent dental/skeletal X-rays; lead apron if unavoidable
  • CT scans: higher radiation — prefer MRI (no ionizing radiation) unless essential
  • Occupational: request reassignment from radiology/nuclear medicine/aviation jobs
  • Airport security scanners: pregnant women can request manual screening
★ Exam Q: What radiation sources can a pregnant woman avoid?
  • Non-urgent dental or skeletal X-rays (postpone; lead apron if essential)
  • CT scans — prefer MRI (no ionizing radiation)
  • Radiology/nuclear medicine/aviation workplaces — request reassignment
  • Airport body scanners — request manual screening

Principle: ALARA — As Low As Reasonably Achievable.

Chemicals & Pollutants
Heavy metals · Pesticides · Food additives
⚠️ Lead · Mercury · Zinc — physical deformities
AdditivesFood, pesticides, cosmetics — currently unknown effects on prenatal development Heavy metalsLead, zinc, mercury → physical deformities & mental retardation in embryo/fetus BreastfeedingExposure during AND after pregnancy/breastfeeding → neurological & pathological damage Paternal effectMay damage father's chromosomes → miscarriages or genetic defects in offspring
  • Heavy metals (lead, zinc, mercury) → physical deformities in embryo/fetus
  • Mental retardation from heavy metal exposure
  • Neurological damage from post-birth exposure (dose effect)
  • Paternal chromosome damage → miscarriages or genetic defects
  • Unknown long-term effects from common additives (pesticides, cosmetics)
  • Avoid high-mercury fish (shark, swordfish, king mackerel, tilefish)
  • Avoid lead paint in older homes (renovation work especially)
  • Minimize pesticide exposure; choose organic when feasible
  • Avoid occupational chemical exposure — inform employer of pregnancy
  • Risk continues after birth through breastfeeding — persistence matters
★ Key heavy metals: Lead, Zinc, Mercury → physical deformities + mental retardation. Important: harm continues after birth via breastfeeding (dose effect). Also notable: paternal exposure can damage chromosomes → miscarriage or genetic defects in offspring — an often-overlooked paternal teratogen effect.
